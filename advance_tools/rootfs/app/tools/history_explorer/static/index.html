How History Explorer works

Where the data comes from

Everything here is read from Home Assistant's recorder — the database HA writes state changes into. This tool never stores history of its own, so your recorder settings decide how far back you can look and which entities exist at all. If an entity is excluded from the recorder, it will be empty here no matter what range you pick.

Raw history vs long-term statistics

The recorder keeps two very different things, and this tool picks between them for you:

  • Raw history — every single state change, exactly as it happened. Used for ranges up to 48 hours. Full detail, but a month of it would be millions of rows.
  • Long-term statistics — pre-computed hourly and daily summaries that survive recorder purges. Used for longer ranges. Each point is an average over its bucket, so a two-minute spike is smoothed away.

The line under the chart always tells you which one you are looking at. You can override it with the Resolution selector — just remember that asking for raw history over 30 days will be slow, and that only numeric sensors have statistics at all. Switches, lights and binary sensors are always read from raw history.

Why there are gaps in the line

A break in a line is real information, not a rendering bug. It means the entity reported unavailable or unknown, or simply reported nothing at all — the device was off, restarting, or out of range. Those moments are deliberately not drawn as zero: a thermostat that goes unavailable did not suddenly read 0°.

An entity that only appears halfway through the range is the same story — it simply had no history before that point.

Down-sampling

A series longer than 1500 points is thinned server-side before it is sent, keeping the first and last point of every unbroken run and never closing a gap. When that happens the chart says so. The statistics are computed on the same points you see.

How the numbers are computed

  • Mean is time-weighted: a value that held for six hours counts more than one that held for six seconds. That is what makes it comparable across irregular sampling.
  • Delta is simply last − first over the range.
  • On-time counts every second spent in an "on"-like state (on, open, home, unlocked, playing, detected…). Time spent unavailable counts towards neither on nor off.
  • Transitions is how many times the state actually changed inside the range.

CSV export

⬇ CSV gives you the same data as the chart: one row per timestamp, one column per entity, ISO-8601 timestamps in your Home Assistant time zone. Blank cells mean that entity had nothing to report at that instant — they are not zeros.
